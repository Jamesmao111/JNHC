The why and the how

Edward Bernays believed the public was an “irrational herd of sheep” that could be easily manipulated.He held the belief that consent and public voice were not expressed but contructed.To ensure the dominance of his clients corporate objectives he emploiyed three primary techniques, the strategic utilisation of seemingly independent fornmt groups, strategic weaponisation of phycological labels and information saturation.This alienated dissenting voices creating idgid boiundaries no one was brave enough to cross.

Main methods of manipulation

  • Creating false consensus through mass media and social norms
  • Using deep rooted phycological fears and desires to manipulate the public
  • Using seemingly independent societies to generate credibility for his client’s beliefs and perspectives

Information flooding and saturation

In a free country it was widely recognised as a civilian figure directly engaging and suppressing information could be counterproductive, instead of achieving censorship.This caused the creation of the information saturation strategy, often referred to as “the engineering of consent”.

Information saturation was the systematic approach to mass persuasion by utilising every media outlet imaginable to create a continuous commercial narrative rather than simple advertising.

Drowning out dissenting voices by flooding the media with a relentless feed of misinformation and pre-prepared, engaging and entertaining corporate material advocating for Edward Bernays's clients.

This content was a major contrubutor to media revenue, angencies heavy relience on this content naturally prioritised these packages to remain sustainable

Directely causinmg genuine voices and adviocates to be crowded out of the spotlight a classical demonstration of this concept may be the “All American Eggs and Bacon breakfast”.

All American Eggs and Bacon breakfast

The classical eggs and bacon American breakfast is a prime example of information floodimng through the media to encompass the general public in one common voice, perspective and commercial narrative.

In the 1920s a typical American breakfast was light consisting of roll and coffee which negatively impacted the sales of protien heavy food stuff including bacon

This resulted in Edward Bernays directely targeting people's rellience on doctors and medical proffessionals through a questionnaire inquring about light and heavy breakfasts.Newspapers, magazines and articles were saturated with survey results consisting of almost 5000 physicians with signitures and statements testifying for the benefits of a heavy breakfast on behalf of the Nut-Beech company to increase public consumption of bacon.The overwhelming velocity at which this information was injected into the media overshadowed genuine voices and advocates.Whom where crowded out of the spotlight.

This clearly demonstrates the effects of information saturation in the media and it's ability to unanimousely engineer the perspective of bacon

Manipulation of front groups

Edward Bernays pioneered the utization of “front groups”, seeminly indeoendant, civic minded organisations claiming to speak for the public, with a genuine interest in thier opinion and well being.

When in reality they were funded to align with corporate interests.

Real grassroots found themselves effectively netralised and silenced as their plateforms were co-opted and hijacked by these disingenuous “mimics” through their power corporate financial backing.

This blurred the lines of advocacy made it increasingly difficult for the general public to differentiate the difference between a real genuine advocates and a corporate artificial voices manufactured by a corporate campaign.An event such like this may be The temperature Research Foundation.

The temperature research foundation seves as a classical example of front groups repersenting corporate interests

Weaponsising Authority to enforce phycological labels and stigma

When critical Voices break through the saturation of information, instead of directly engaging in an debate against the speaker he would undermine their credibility. His primary tool or stratedgy to combat dissenting voices was the strategic deployment of seemingly independent authoroties such as the voices of physicians, scientists and academic instituteto align with corporate or marketing interests. Edward Bernay’s marketed his clients objectives as picture perfect undisputed social, technological and scientific progress labelling anyone whom opinions differed as unscientific, fringe and stupid. This method generated a powerful stigma forcing or indirectly suppressing voices by forcing critics to remain silent to avoid the public alienation.For example the flouridatio campiegn succesfully rebranded a toxic by product by silencing dissenting voices for a company's own selfish economic gain.

Flouridation Campaign

In the 1940s and 1950s the Aluminium Company of America (Alcoa) was facing massive public relations liability regarding the million of tons of sodium Chlouride they were producing When research suggested sodium fluoride was beneficial towards oral hygiene or dental health, Edward Bernays flooded the media with large trustworthy entities advocating for the benefits of sodium fluoride in water, succesfully winning over the American Health Association and the American Medical Association, rebranding this toxic byproduct. Anyone voice dissenting his ideas were suppressed under enforced stigma choosing the remain silent in order to not be labelled or marginalised by the general public as uneducated, “crackpot”, “communist” or “Anti science eccentric” in order to avoid social and professional isolation and alienation.
